A gap at 22 doesn't stay small.

Raises, bonuses, and your next job's offer are almost always calculated as a percentage of what you make now. Accept a few thousand under market at graduation, and you don't lose it once — you lose it every year, compounding, for an entire career.

One conversation at the start changes the whole curve. Drag the slider — the gap is real money, and it's yours to keep.

Why Georgia Tech

GT publishes salary data. There's a catch.

Every semester, Georgia Tech surveys graduates and publishes their reported salaries. It's a genuinely useful snapshot — but look at the fine print.

"Results are based on self-reported information... actual salaries vary by experience, location, and other factors."

Not a verified benchmark

Georgia Tech Career Survey Report, Office of Academic Effectiveness, 2025 (paraphrased)

Self-reported numbers are what students remember, round up, or guess. Nobody checks them against a real market source. So when you're staring at an offer letter at 11pm wondering "is this good?" — the data you'd need to answer that question doesn't exist here yet. That's the gap this initiative closes.

The Fix

Two tools. One fair start.

The Fair Start Initiative asks the Career Center to build one resource with two connected parts — real data, and the skills to use it.

PART 01

Verified pay bands

Role-specific, location-adjusted salary ranges for the most common Georgia Tech graduate destinations — built from sources that can actually be trusted:

  • Free public wage data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics
  • One professionally verified compensation subscription
  • Zero crowdsourced, self-reported guesswork

PART 02

A negotiation playbook

A short, plain-language guide that turns the data into a conversation you can actually have:

  • How to read a pay band and spot a below-market offer
  • How to frame a counteroffer against the midpoint
  • Where your internships and experience place you in the band
  • Why benefits — often worth ~30% more — are negotiable even when base pay isn't

The Plan

One semester, four phases.

This isn't a moonshot. It's a pilot the Career Center could run start-to-finish inside a single semester.

Weeks 1–3

Source the data

Lock in BLS feeds, the verified subscription, and partnerships with the Career Center and Office of Academic Effectiveness.

Weeks 4–7

Build the tools

Compile the pay bands and write the negotiation playbook in plain student language.

Weeks 8–11

Pilot with grads

Test with one graduating cohort, gather baseline metrics, refine what's confusing.

Weeks 12–15

Launch campus-wide

Roll out through orientation, advising appointments, and digital signage.

Fair Questions

Asked by actual skeptics.

Isn't this data already on Glassdoor or Levels.fyi?

Those sites are self-reported — anonymous users typing in numbers nobody verifies. They're fine for a vibe check, terrible for a negotiation. Fair Start uses Bureau of Labor Statistics data plus a professionally verified compensation source, so the band you see is one you can actually cite in a conversation with a recruiter.

Why does GT need to pay for data at all?

The free BLS data is excellent but broad. One verified subscription ($3,500 of the $6,800) adds the role-by-role, city-by-city precision that makes a band useful for a specific offer. It's the difference between "engineers make good money" and "this role, in this city, pays $72k at the midpoint."

Does negotiating risk losing the offer?

A respectful, data-backed counteroffer almost never rescinds an offer — companies expect the conversation. What actually goes wrong is negotiating with no evidence ("I feel like I'm worth more"). The playbook exists precisely so you're never in that position: you anchor to a verified midpoint, not a feeling.

Is this just for business majors?

No — the bands use role levels that generalize across every major: Entry-Level, Specialist, and Senior/Lead. A CS grad, an ISyE grad, and an architecture grad would each see bands for their own destinations. First offers are a campus-wide problem, so the tool is campus-wide too.

Who would actually run this?

The Career Center — which already runs the semesterly Career Survey and already employs advisors who coach students on offers. A funded student research assistant handles band compilation and upkeep. No new hires, no new office. The pilot mostly connects things GT already has.

Isn't GT's existing salary report enough?

It's a great historical snapshot, and this initiative builds on it — but GT's own report notes its numbers are self-reported and vary by experience and location. A snapshot tells you what past students said they got. A verified band tells you what the market pays right now, which is what you need mid-negotiation.
